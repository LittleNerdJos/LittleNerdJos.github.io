Questions to Consider

  1. How fast do the plants spread?
  2. How do the starting spots affect the spread of plants?
  3. What might the 3 landscapes look like?
  4. How predictable is the spread of the plants?
  5. Which landscapes best control the spread of the weeds?
  6. Which weed spreads the slowest? On what landscape?
  7. How many simulations does it take to see patterns?
  8. What are some different ways to measure the spread of weeds?
  9. How well do the simulation results match known information about the plants?

More Information

This app uses a mathematical model to simulate the spread of an invasive plant over time.   The model assumes a landscape is split into a grid of cells, so that each cell has a suitability (determined by the landscape and weed) which determines the probability of spread from an adjacent cell. The model is an example of a stochastic cellular automaton  and is implemented using JavaScript.

Simulators are a cheap and efficient way to generate "experimental data". Scientists use simulations like this to decide how well a mathematical model fits observations in the field, how changes in one or more factors might affect important outcomes, and how much predictions might change under various conditions. This simulator is also a good tool for learning about randomness and spatial spread. Students can use this tool to think about how a scientist might measure the rate at which an invasive species is spreading, as well as how a scientist might make a map for the suitability of a particular landscape for the growth of an invasive plant.

Cheatgrass is a widespread winter annual grass that spreads quickly due to a short lifespan and prolific production of seeds. Cheatgrass dries out early in the summer, leading to earlier and more destructive fires in the West.
Hoary Cress (Whitetop) is a 2-3 foot tall plant that often grows along roadsides and rivers. It can crowd out planted grains like wheat, will harm livestock if consumed, and spreads both through its roots and annual production of seeds.
Medusahead Rye is a winter annual grass that migrated to Oregon from Europe in the late 1800s. The plant germinates in the fall, grows throughout the winter, and produces many seeds in the spring. Dead medusahead plants often accumulate on the ground, slowly breaking down while limiting growth of other plants.
Puncturevine is a low-lying plant that often grows in disturbed or compact soil. Its fruit produces bunches of burs, sometimes called 'goatheads', which are so sharp that they often puncture bike tires and other soft materials.
